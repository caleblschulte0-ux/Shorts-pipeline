# Third Channel — Internet Taste Playbook

This is the content standard for the Twitch/Kick clipping channel.

The north star is not "more editing." It is:

> A stranger understands the human situation immediately, feels something,
> and stays because a clear payoff is coming.

YouTube describes recommendation performance in three buckets: **appeal**
(people choose to watch), **engagement** (they keep watching), and
**satisfaction** (they enjoyed it). Every decision below exists to improve one
of those three buckets.

## 1. What people actually want from streamer clips

The broad internet normally does **not** care about the stream itself. It cares
about recognizable human situations happening on the stream:

1. Someone gets caught, exposed, embarrassed, humbled, or proven wrong.
2. Two people disagree, roast each other, betray each other, or choose sides.
3. Someone is scared, shocked, angry, crying, laughing, or genuinely confused.
4. A challenge, stunt, game, or bet produces a visible win, failure, or reversal.
5. A wholesome interaction feels real rather than staged.
6. A current event or live storyline gives fans a reason to search now.

The clip must work for a person who does not know the streamer, the game, the
inside joke, or what happened earlier in the broadcast.

## 2. The one-sentence test

Before downloading or rendering, describe the clip in one sentence:

> **[Person] tries/does [clear action], but [surprising consequence].**

Examples:

- "Lacy tries to explain the wheelchair fall, but the coach does not believe her."
- "Jason starts a chant, but the entire hallway turns it into chaos."
- "Jynxzi has one chance to tie the game and keeps hitting the post."

Reject the clip when the sentence is only:

- "Streamer reacts."
- "Streamer talks to chat."
- "Funny moment."
- "He loses it."
- "You had to be there."

Those descriptions do not promise a specific payoff.

## 3. The 100-point greenlight score

Score a candidate before expensive transcription and rendering.

| Category | Points | Question |
|---|---:|---|
| Instant clarity | 15 | Can a stranger understand the scene in the first second? |
| Universal stakes | 15 | Is there a conflict, risk, challenge, relationship, or consequence? |
| Real emotion | 15 | Is a person visibly feeling something strong and authentic? |
| Payoff | 15 | Does the clip build to a clear change, reveal, punchline, win, or failure? |
| Visual readability | 10 | Is the subject/action visible without explanation? |
| Complete context | 10 | Does the clip include enough setup and the full reaction afterward? |
| Freshness | 10 | Is it recent or attached to a live event/storyline people care about now? |
| Fan/search pull | 5 | Is there a named person, event, game, or phrase viewers search for? |
| Commentability | 5 | Can viewers honestly take a side, rate it, or predict what should happen? |

**Post at 72+ points.**

**Test cautiously at 62–71.** Only post when supply is weak and the visual
payoff is undeniable.

**Reject below 62.** An empty slot is better than teaching viewers to swipe.

### Automatic rejection rules

Reject regardless of score when any of these is true:

- The payoff is cut off or happens outside the clip.
- The clip begins in the middle of an unexplained reaction.
- No person or clear action is visible for a meaningful stretch.
- The source is dark, blurry, frozen, boxed into a tiny area, or mostly empty.
- The moment depends on an inside joke that cannot be explained naturally.
- It is a sponsor read, giveaway, gifted-sub notification, menu/setup screen,
  routine gameplay, or ordinary conversation with no change.
- The title needs to exaggerate or invent an event to sound interesting.
- It is materially the same event or emotional beat as another post that day.

## 4. Preferred content mix

For a normal slate, target:

- **50% human drama:** disagreements, caught/exposed, embarrassment, authority
  conflict, relationship tension, roasting, real reactions.
- **20% challenge/fail/win:** a visually obvious attempt with a result.
- **15% surprise or fear:** reveals, jumpscares, unexpected reversals.
- **10% wholesome:** sincere support, generosity, reunion, respect.
- **5% gameplay-only:** only when the action is instantly legible and exceptional.

This is a target, not a quota. Never force a weak clip to fill a bucket.

## 5. The opening

The first second must show either:

- the cause of the moment,
- the person about to make the decision,
- or the most visually understandable part of the conflict.

Do not begin on:

- silence,
- a logo or title card,
- an empty room,
- a streamer staring at a screen,
- the middle of screaming with no visible cause,
- or a three-second hook card that delays the actual clip.

Use text to clarify the situation, not replace it. A strong opening caption is a
small context label such as:

- `THE COACH DIDN'T BELIEVE HER`
- `HE ONLY NEEDED ONE GOAL`
- `CHAT HAD ALREADY SNITCHED`

The clip itself should begin underneath it immediately.

## 6. Cut and length

There is no magic universal duration. Use the shortest complete version of the
story.

Typical target: **12–28 seconds**.

Keep:

1. Minimum setup needed to understand the situation.
2. The escalation or attempt.
3. The complete payoff.
4. Roughly 0.8–2.0 seconds of genuine reaction after the payoff.

Remove repeated explanation, dead air, menus, walking, throat-clearing, and
anything that does not change the viewer's understanding or anticipation.

Never cut so aggressively that the video becomes confusing. Completion without
context is not good retention; it is accidental looping.

## 7. Editing rules

Editing should make the moment easier to consume, not announce that software
edited it.

Always useful:

- accurate, readable captions;
- a stable crop that keeps the important person/action visible;
- clean audio;
- removing dead time;
- one intentional emphasis at the actual payoff.

Use sparingly:

- punch zooms;
- sound effects;
- replay;
- color flashes;
- shake;
- emojis;
- transition hits.

Do not place an effect on every cut. Repetition makes the effect feel automated
and can cover the authentic reaction viewers came to see.

## 8. Titles that earn both search and curiosity

Use:

> **[Specific person] + [specific action/conflict] + [real consequence]**

Good shapes:

- `Lacy Falls In The Wheelchair And The Coach Doesn't Buy Her Excuse`
- `Jason Starts A Free Jason Chant And The Hallway Takes It Too Far`
- `Jynxzi Keeps Hitting The Post With The Match On The Line`

Weak shapes:

- `Streamer Loses It`
- `This Was Crazy`
- `You Won't Believe What Happened`
- `Funniest Twitch Clip Ever`

Rules:

- Name the person viewers recognize or search for.
- State the concrete situation.
- Preserve one honest unanswered question.
- Avoid duplicate naming such as `Jasontheween: Jason...` unless the second
  person is genuinely different.
- Do not overuse "after this," "goes off the rails," or "takes a wild turn."
  Template repetition makes the channel look machine-generated.
- Use the exact event/storyline phrase when search traffic proves people use it.

## 9. Hooks and calls to action

A hook is context, not clickbait. It should clarify the stakes in 3–7 words.

A call to action should be visible where Shorts viewers can encounter it:

- a brief final on-screen question;
- a pinned comment;
- or a natural question in the caption.

Good prompts:

- `Was the coach being fair?`
- `Does that count as a choke?`
- `Who was actually wrong here?`
- `Rate the reaction 1–10.`

Do not manufacture conflict. Do not ask a question when the answer is obvious
or unrelated to the clip.

## 10. Posting strategy

Until the feedback loop is statistically trustworthy:

- Post **3–4 strong clips per day**, not eight average clips.
- Usually cap one streamer at one post per day; allow a second only for a
  separate major event.
- Space posts far enough apart to measure them independently.
- Keep the successful search wording while testing stronger feed openings.
- Change one major variable at a time: selection, opening, structure, or CTA.
- Do not call an experiment after a handful of low-view videos.

## 11. Metrics that decide what to make next

Judge mature public videos, not scheduled/private uploads.

### Appeal

- Shown in feed.
- Chose to view versus swiped away.
- Search impressions and clicks for the exact person/event.

### Engagement

- First-two-second survival.
- Average view duration.
- Average percentage viewed, capped or down-weighted when the sample is tiny.
- Completion and replay behavior.

### Satisfaction

- Likes per engaged view.
- Comments per engaged view.
- Shares.
- Subscribers gained.
- Whether the same viewers watch another clip from the channel.

### Minimum evidence before learning from a video

Do not use a video to train streamer or format priors until it has at least:

- 24 hours public,
- 50 total views,
- and 25 engaged views.

Below that threshold, label the result **insufficient evidence**. A 600% average
view percentage from one looping viewer is not a winning content strategy.

## 12. Daily editorial questions

Before approving a slate, answer:

1. What exact emotion does each clip create?
2. What changes between the beginning and end?
3. Can a stranger understand it without streamer lore?
4. Is the payoff visible and complete?
5. Why would somebody comment or send it to a friend?
6. Are we posting a genuinely different moment, or another version of the same
   screaming/roasting clip?
7. Would the title still sound interesting without "crazy," "wild," "loses it,"
   or "after this"?

When those answers are weak, reject the clip before rendering.
